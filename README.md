# Kalshi — International Market Entry Framework

An interactive decision framework for evaluating and prioritizing international market entry opportunities.

**Live:** [katherinehu24.github.io/kalshi-framework](https://katherinehu24.github.io/kalshi-framework)

---

## Why I built this

Kalshi's October 2025 global launch — 140 countries, announced alongside the Series D — didn't fully land. Users in India, Nigeria, and Brazil reported they couldn't sign up. The app stayed US-only. Volume didn't move.

That gap between the announcement and the reality is the actual problem. And it's not a technology problem. It's that access without regulatory cover recreates the "offshore gambling site" narrative Kalshi is trying to escape.

This framework is an attempt to think through what a real market entry process looks like — starting with the question that determines everything else.

---

## The framework

Five gates, in priority order:

**Gate 1 — Derivatives classification** *(kill gate)*
Can event contracts be classified as financial derivatives — not gambling — under this country's legal framework? Kalshi's CFTC designation is US-specific. Every new market needs its own legal theory. Without one, nothing else matters.

**Gate 2 — Regulatory ownership** *(kill gate)*
One regulator or two competing for jurisdiction? The US state-level fights — Nevada, Massachusetts, Ohio — play out internationally too, without home-court advantage. Two hostile regulators with no established precedent is a reason to wait, not push.

**Gate 3 — The Brazil test**
Does a credible local licensed partner exist? The XP Inc. structure — local partner owns regulatory relationships, Kalshi provides exchange technology — is the only path that scales. Direct licensing across 40 markets isn't a strategy.

**Gate 4 — Sports sequencing**
Should this market launch non-sports first? Sports is 89% of US revenue but the first thing that triggers a gambling regulator internationally. In most markets the right sequence is: financial/macro events first to establish the derivatives precedent, sports later once the classification question is settled.

**Gate 5 — Market quality**
Active investor base × event density. Population is a bad proxy. What matters is how many people already trade financial products, and how often high-stakes contested events occur.

---

## Three views

- **Walk through a market** — answer five questions, get a prioritization verdict
- **Compare markets** — side-by-side scoring across seven countries
- **Framework logic** — full reasoning per gate, including kill conditions

---

## Where this breaks down

Gate 4 assumes non-sports sequencing is always safer. In markets where sports betting is already legally settled — Australia, UK — that probably inverts. Sports may be the faster path to regulatory credibility there because it's a known, licensed category.

The partner scoring is too binary. The difference between a weak partner and no partner matters operationally and the model doesn't capture it.

The framework also evaluates markets independently. It doesn't model which entry creates regulatory precedent that makes the next one easier — Switzerland to Germany, Australia to New Zealand. That sequencing logic is missing and is probably where the most interesting thinking lives.
